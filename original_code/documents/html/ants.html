Project overview: The Communicating Ants

One of the issues we're dealing with in relation to mobile agents is that of cooperation amongst agents so as to use as few resources as possible while obtaining the maximum possible benefit. Problems of this type will find application in information retrieval and a variety of other agent-based processes. Resource utilization would involve things such as the cost of sending messages and moving around a network, while benefits might be measured in terms of obtaining useful information from a site or performing some preassigned task.

This project (the communicating ants) involves testing a number of modes of optimization for learning optimal behavior in a simulated environment. The world we use is several steps removed from being an actual network simulation. In our simulation, digital "ants" wander around a 2-dimensional, toroidal world, looking for food. They can:

  • communicate their location
  • listen for incoming communications
  • eat (if on food)
  • teleport to the location from which communication was last received
  • move randomly (within a local area), or
  • do nothing.

Defining states for the "communicating ants" problem

In any possible simulation, the ants will choose their optimal actions based on their state. There are two competing objectives: we either (1) operate with a small state space, and optimize quickly, or (2) use a large, extremely precise state space and accept that we may take a long time to learn our optimal poilicy within each state. Therefore, our simulations allow a complete spectrum of options, all of which have a known number of states. Our problem is initially one of defining what states we are to consider, and second to examine how the actions and costs will affect our choice of actions.

It is natural to first consider the state of a single ant. We can imagine our ants as having energy, quantized in some fashion. It might be as simple as "near dead" or "feeling good," or perhaps an ordered scale from 1 to 100. The ants might also have some preference as to which direction they might like to travel, including whether they’d like to travel at all. High-cost actions will generally be avoided, especially if they are high cost-to-go and not just high initial cost. Other state information a single ant might retain is some indication of how many other ants are at their location, so that they can know how good a job they've done in cooperating (more on this later).

Death of the fittest

If we want cooperation to be possible, let alone desirable, there must be a way for ants that communicate to "get ahead," so to speak. This ties in with the mode of optimization: if we optimize by genetic programming, there must be a way to ensure the survival of the impetus to communicate helpful information. If we operate on the idea that ants which do the best job of "watching out for number 1" will survive, then surely an ant that wastes energy telling his competitors the whereabouts of his food supply will not survive long enough to have children that do the same. Likewise, in Q-Learning, the high cost of communicating will quickly drive that action from the optimal policy. Either policy leaves us with "rugged individualists" that see communication as a high-cost way of killing oneself by attracting competition.

Programming altruism

The incorporation of communication as a beneficial action therefore requires that we look beyond the ant who may be considering letting his fellows in on the secret stockpile of food he’s found. We are not nearly so concerned with the ant that chose to communicate as we are with the population at large which benefited from the communication. In a sense, we should treat small groups of cooperating ants as single organisms, in competition with other groups. The ant groups that communicate the most effectively should survive better than those that don’t. In this sense, we may use a "survival of the fittest" approach, only it will now be the fit groups that survive rather than the fit individuals.

The question of defining state based on a collection of individual states is rather like the competition within economic systems. Corporations, for the most part, are internally cooperative. That is, the individuals within an ideal corporation will do whatever is necessary to support the corporate goal. However, this same body is in fierce competition with other corporations. The other corporations may or may not be internally cooperative: that is their own business. The group with the better internal cooperation will quite possibly outperform that with greater internal resources. In this sense, we seek to evolve strategies for cooperation rather than strategies for exploitation of one’s cooperatives.
